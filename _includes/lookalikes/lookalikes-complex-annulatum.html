# Lookalikes { .collapse } This lookalikes block covers the *Sphagnum annulatum* complex in Europe: *S. annulatum*, *S. jensenii*, *S. balticum* and *S. majus*. With field experience, these can often be separated by {% include tooltip.html term="capitulum" text="capitulum" %} structure, but the species can overlap in macromorphology along the "dry-wet" {% include tooltip.html term="ecogradient" text="ecogradient" %}, so microscopy can be required to be sure.1 *S. annulatum* is a parent species of *S. jensenii* (together with *S. balticum*) and of *S. majus* (together with *S. cuspidatum*). ## At a glance (field / hand lens) - **S. annulatum:** usually the most clearly {% include tooltip.html term="stellate" text="stellate" %}, with a distinct exposed {% include tooltip.html term="apical bud" text="apical bud" %}; chestnut gloss and arcuato-decurved branches are supportive but can overlap with lookalikes.1 2 - **S. jensenii:** usually the largest and most variable across wetness; the apical bud is often partly concealed, and branch-leaf arrangement is typically less {% include tooltip.html term="imbricate" text="imbricate" %} and less clearly {% include tooltip.html term="ranked" text="ranked" %}.1 3 - **S. balticum:** usually flatter-headed, with straighter branches and more clearly {% include tooltip.html term="ranked" text="ranked" %} branch leaves; often in nutrient-poor mire habitats, including both {% include tooltip.html term="ombrotrophic" text="ombrotrophic" %} and {% include tooltip.html term="minerotrophic" text="minerotrophic" %} settings.1 4 - **S. majus:** usually less clearly stellate, with a more domed head, laterally curved branches, and an apical bud that is often indistinct or hidden.1 5 - **Practical caution:** habitat and number of {% include tooltip.html term="pendent branches" text="pendent branches" %} can support an ID but are not decisive alone; confirm with species notes and microscopy when needed.1 ## Species notes (paraphrased from sources) *S. annulatum*
**Key field marks (summary)** - Usually smaller and less robust than *S. jensenii*, with a more clearly {% include tooltip.html term="stellate" text="stellate" %} head and a distinct, exposed {% include tooltip.html term="apical bud" text="apical bud" %}.1 2 - Divergent branches are often more distinctly {% include tooltip.html term="arcuato decurved" text="arcuato-decurved" %}; branch leaves tend to be more {% include tooltip.html term="imbricate" text="imbricate" %}, more {% include tooltip.html term="concave surface" text="concave" %}, and more reduced near the {% include tooltip.html term="proximal" text="proximal" %} end than in *S. jensenii*.1 - Stem leaves of *S. annulatum* are usually described as more elongate (relatively narrower and longer), whereas *S. jensenii* and *S. balticum* are often described as broader/shorter and more concave in these comparisons; treat this as supportive because shape can vary with habitat.2 3 4 - Versus *S. balticum*, look for the clearer apical bud and more arcuato-decurved, more narrowly tapering ({% include tooltip.html term="attenuate" text="attenuate" %}) outer branches.1 2 ### *S. annulatum* vs *S. jensenii* { .collapse } - *S. annulatum* is usually less robust, with a clearer exposed apical bud and a more regularly 5-radiate head than *S. jensenii*.1 2 - Divergent branch leaves in *S. annulatum* are typically more imbricate, less disorderly, more concave, and more strongly reduced near the {% include tooltip.html term="proximal" text="proximal" %} end of the branch.1 - Stem {% include tooltip.html term="cortex" text="cortex" %} is often weakly differentiated or nearly undifferentiated in *S. annulatum*, unlike the clearer differentiation in *S. jensenii*.1 ### *S. annulatum* vs *S. balticum* { .collapse } - *S. annulatum* more often shows a distinct apical bud, a more stellate head, and more arcuato-decurved outer branches than *S. balticum*.1 2 - Outer branches in *S. annulatum* are more narrowly tapering, whereas *S. balticum* tends to be flatter with straighter, more obtusely tapering branches.1 4 - Habitat overlap can occur in transitional fen zones, so combine morphology with habitat rather than relying on habitat alone.1 2 ### *S. annulatum* vs *S. majus* { .collapse } - *S. annulatum* is typically more clearly stellate, with a more distinct apical bud and straighter (less laterally curved) overall structure than *S. majus*.1 5 - Divergent branches in *S. annulatum* lack the distal sigmoid tendency often described for *S. majus*; branch leaves are usually more imbricate and less irregular.1 ### Microscopy (key separators for *S. annulatum*) { .collapse } - In divergent branch leaves, hyalocysts are usually of more uniform length between basal and middle portions (contrasting with the stronger basal elongation in *S. jensenii*).1 - On the {% include tooltip.html term="convex surface" text="convex surface" %}, commissural {% include tooltip.html term="pseudopore" text="pseudopores" %} are usually rare in *S. annulatum* (commoner in *S. jensenii*), while {% include tooltip.html term="ringed pore" text="ringed pores" %} are more frequently present than in *S. jensenii* and *S. majus*.1 - On the concave surface of divergent branch leaves, many pores are typical in *S. annulatum* (contrasting with mostly pore-poor *S. majus* subsp. *majus*).1 - Pore traits vary with habitat and sex, so use pore features together with macromorphology for reliable separation.1 *S. jensenii*
**Key field marks (summary)** - Usually more robust than *S. annulatum* and more variable along the wetness gradient, so habit alone can be misleading across habitats.1 - The {% include tooltip.html term="apical bud" text="apical bud" %} is often shorter than the inner branches and partly concealed; the head is often less distinctly {% include tooltip.html term="stellate" text="stellate" %} than in *S. annulatum*.1 2 - Divergent branch leaves are typically less {% include tooltip.html term="imbricate" text="imbricate" %}, more irregularly arranged and less {% include tooltip.html term="concave surface" text="concave" %} than in *S. annulatum*.1 2 - Compared with *S. balticum*, *S. jensenii* is usually larger with a more distinct apical bud and less obtusely tapering outer branches.1 4 ### *S. jensenii* vs *S. annulatum* { .collapse } - *S. jensenii* is generally more robust and less regularly 5-radiate, with a less exposed apical bud; *S. annulatum* is usually smaller with a clearer exposed bud.1 2 - Divergent branch leaves in *S. jensenii* are less imbricate, more disorderly and less concave, and are less reduced near the {% include tooltip.html term="proximal" text="proximal" %} end of the branch.1 - Stem {% include tooltip.html term="cortex" text="cortex" %} is more clearly differentiated in *S. jensenii*; in *S. annulatum* it is often weakly differentiated or nearly undifferentiated.1 ### *S. jensenii* vs *S. balticum* { .collapse } - *S. jensenii* is typically more robust, with larger heads and a more distinct apical bud than *S. balticum*.1 4 - Outer branches in *S. jensenii* are usually less straight and less obtusely tapering; branch leaves are less ranked/imbricate than in *S. balticum*.1 - Similarity to *S. balticum* is most problematic in uncommon drier hummock situations; in more typical carpet habitat, separation is usually easier, but use multiple characters rather than a single field trait.1 ### *S. jensenii* vs *S. majus* { .collapse } - *S. jensenii* is typically more robust, with less laterally curved branches and a more distinct apical bud; *S. majus* often appears more laterally curved and less clearly stellate.1 5 - Divergent branch leaves in *S. jensenii* are usually less {% include tooltip.html term="subsecund" text="subsecund" %} and less {% include tooltip.html term="involute" text="involute" %} than in *S. majus*.1 ### Microscopy (key separators for *S. jensenii*) { .collapse } - In divergent branch leaves, hyalocysts are often markedly longer in the basal part than in the middle part; this contrasts with the more uniform pattern in *S. annulatum*.1 - On the {% include tooltip.html term="convex surface" text="convex surface" %}, commissural {% include tooltip.html term="pseudopore" text="pseudopores" %} are commonly present (especially apically), while they are rare in *S. annulatum* and only occasional in *S. majus*.1 - On the concave surface of divergent branch leaves, many pores are usually present (contrasting with typically pore-poor *S. majus* subsp. *majus*).1 - Pore number and size vary with habitat and sex, so combine pore traits with branch/leaf architecture for reliable separation.1 *S. balticum*
**Key field marks (summary)** - Usually flatter-looking and more slender than *S. jensenii*, with a less distinct (often hidden) apical bud in this complex.1 2 4 - Outer capitulum branches tend to look straighter and more {% include tooltip.html term="obtuse" text="obtusely" %} tapering than those of *S. jensenii* and especially *S. annulatum*.1 4 - Divergent branch leaves are often more clearly {% include tooltip.html term="ranked" text="ranked" %} and more imbricate; stem leaves are described as shorter, broader and more concave than in *S. annulatum*.1 2 - Most typical in nutrient-poor mire vegetation, and common in both {% include tooltip.html term="ombrotrophic" text="ombrotrophic" %} and {% include tooltip.html term="minerotrophic" text="minerotrophic" %} settings; treat habitat as supportive rather than decisive.1 2 ### *S. balticum* vs *S. annulatum* { .collapse } - *S. balticum* typically has a flatter head and less distinct apical bud, whereas *S. annulatum* more often shows a distinct exposed apical bud.1 2 - Outer branches in *S. balticum* are usually straighter and more {% include tooltip.html term="obtuse" text="obtusely" %} tapering; *S. annulatum* is more arcuato-decurved and more narrowly tapering ({% include tooltip.html term="attenuate" text="attenuate" %}).1 2 - Stem leaves in *S. balticum* are described as shorter/broader and more concave than in *S. annulatum* in comparison texts.2 4 ### *S. balticum* vs *S. jensenii* { .collapse } - *S. balticum* is usually less robust, with a flatter capitulum and a less distinct apical bud; *S. jensenii* is typically larger, with a more distinct apical bud.1 4 - Divergent branch leaves in *S. balticum* are generally more ranked/imbricate, while *S. jensenii* tends to show less imbricate, more disorderly leaf arrangement.1 - The number of {% include tooltip.html term="pendent branches" text="pendent branches" %} (often one vs two) varies with habitat in both taxa and is not reliable on its own.1 4 ### *S. balticum* vs *S. majus* { .collapse } - *S. balticum* tends to look flatter with straighter branches/leaves, while *S. majus* is usually less clearly stellate with more laterally curved branches and a more irregular habit.1 5 - In *S. balticum*, one pendent branch is common in drier, nutrient-poor habitats, but counts vary between one and two; in *S. majus*, pendent branch is more often two.1 5 ### Microscopy (key separators for *S. balticum*) { .collapse } - Compared with *S. jensenii*, *S. balticum* usually has fewer hyalocyst pores on the {% include tooltip.html term="convex surface" text="convex surface" %} of divergent branch leaves.1 - {% include tooltip.html term="chlorocyst" text="Chlorocysts" %} in cross section tend to be somewhat more ovate / less ovate-triangular than in *S. jensenii* in comparison material.1 - Pore traits vary with habitat; use pore number and type together with macromorphology rather than as a single decisive character.1 *S. majus* (subsp. *majus*)
**Key field marks (summary)** - Usually a less clearly {% include tooltip.html term="stellate" text="stellate" %}, more domed head with laterally curved capitulum branches and an {% include tooltip.html term="apical bud" text="apical bud" %} that is usually indistinct or hidden.1 5 - Branches and branch leaves often look more irregularly bent, with leaves tending to be more {% include tooltip.html term="subsecund" text="subsecund" %} and {% include tooltip.html term="involute" text="involute" %} and less densely {% include tooltip.html term="imbricate" text="imbricate" %} than in other members of this complex.1 2 - Similar in robustness to *S. annulatum* (though *S. annulatum* can look more slender), less robust than *S. jensenii*; stem leaves are characteristically narrow and divergent branch leaves relatively long.1 - In *S. annulatum*, *S. jensenii*, and *S. balticum*, the number of {% include tooltip.html term="pendent branches" text="pendent branches" %} per fascicle overlaps between one and two and has limited diagnostic value (with habitat-related tendencies); in *S. majus*, pendent branch number also varies from one to two.1 ### *S. majus* vs *S. annulatum* { .collapse } - *S. majus* is often less clearly stellate with more laterally curved capitulum branches, and it often lacks a distinct exposed apical bud.1 5 - Divergent branches in *S. majus* often show a distal sigmoid curve (not emphasized for *S. annulatum*), and branch leaves are described as narrower, more spreading and less densely imbricate in comparison texts.1 2 ### *S. majus* vs *S. jensenii* { .collapse } - *S. majus* is described as less clearly stellate with more laterally curved branches, while *S. jensenii* is flatter-looking with longer and straighter outer branches in these comparisons.5 - Branch leaves in *S. majus* are described as more {% include tooltip.html term="subsecund" text="subsecund" %} and {% include tooltip.html term="involute" text="involute" %}; *S. jensenii* is generally more robust overall.1 5 ### *S. majus* vs *S. balticum* { .collapse } - *S. majus* is described as less clearly stellate with more laterally curved capitulum branches, while *S. balticum* is flatter-looking with straighter branch leaves in these comparisons.5 - Comparison texts also emphasize that *S. balticum* often has one pendent branch in drier, nutrient-poor habitats but more often two in wetter, richer sites, whereas *S. majus* shows pendent branch numbers varying from one to two.5 1 ### Subspecies note: subsp. *norvegicum* vs subsp. *majus* { .collapse } - Artsdatabanken contrasts subsp. *norvegicum* with subsp. *majus* as having lighter, greyer heads, broader branch leaves, and broader stem leaves with a more {% include tooltip.html term="obtuse" text="obtuse" %} apex.5 ### Microscopy (key separators for *S. majus*) { .collapse } - Stem {% include tooltip.html term="cortex" text="cortex" %} is more differentiated than in *S. annulatum*.1 - On the {% include tooltip.html term="convex surface" text="convex surface" %} of divergent branch leaves, *S. majus* typically has fewer, larger and less regularly arranged perfect pores than *S. annulatum*; {% include tooltip.html term="ringed pore" text="ringed perfect pores" %} are absent or rare in the apical portion (common in *S. annulatum*).1 - Compared with *S. jensenii*, {% include tooltip.html term="hyalocyst" text="hyalocyst" %} pores on the convex surface are somewhat larger in *S. majus*, and commissural {% include tooltip.html term="pseudopore" text="pseudopores" %} are only occasional (common in *S. jensenii*).1 - On the concave surface of divergent branch leaves, pores are described as lacking or nearly so in *S. majus*, versus many pores in *S. annulatum* and *S. jensenii*.1 - {% include tooltip.html term="chlorocyst" text="Chlorocysts" %} in {% include tooltip.html term="cross section" text="cross section" %} are usually trapezoidal-rectangular and exposed or only slightly enclosed on the concave side; in *S. annulatum* they are smaller/more ovate, and in *S. jensenii* narrower and more ovate-triangular with broader enclosure on the concave side.1 - Stem leaf hyalocysts are more {% include tooltip.html term="fibrillose" text="fibrillose" %} than in *S. jensenii* (which has less fibrillose and more porose hyalocysts in upper stem leaves); stem leaf cell and pore structure is described as similar between *S. majus* and *S. annulatum*.1 - Pore structure varies with habitat: in similar habitats, *S. annulatum* tends to show the most numerous/smallest pores, *S. jensenii* intermediate, and *S. majus* the fewest/largest; some subsp. *majus* plants can approach *S. annulatum* on convex-surface pore size/number but not on concave-surface pores, where *S. majus* remains nearly poreless.1 ## Microscopy (when macromorphology overlaps) In doubtful cases, microscopy is emphasized; for *S. annulatum*, *S. jensenii*, *S. balticum*, and *S. majus*, use combinations rather than a single character in isolation.1 Pore traits can vary with habitat and (in some cases) sex; interpret them as a combination of characters rather than relying on a single pore count or pore type in isolation.1 1. Compare the relative length of {% include tooltip.html term="hyalocyst" text="hyalocysts" %} in divergent branch leaves: markedly longer hyalocysts in the basal part than in the middle part supports *S. jensenii*; more uniform hyalocyst length supports *S. annulatum*.1 2. Stem {% include tooltip.html term="cortex" text="cortex" %} differentiation in {% include tooltip.html term="cross section" text="cross section" %}: *S. annulatum* is characterized by an undifferentiated or poorly differentiated cortex, whereas *S. jensenii* and *S. majus* are described as having more clearly differentiated cortical cells.1 3. On the convex surface of divergent branch leaves: commissural {% include tooltip.html term="pseudopore" text="pseudopores" %} are described as rare in *S. annulatum* but common in *S. jensenii*; {% include tooltip.html term="ringed pore" text="ringed pores" %} are described as more commonly occurring in *S. annulatum* than in *S. jensenii* and *S. majus*.1 4. On the concave surface of divergent branch leaves: many pores are described for *S. annulatum* and *S. jensenii* compared with *S. majus*, where pores are described as lacking or nearly so in typical material. Contrary to many reports, *S. majus* can show pores on the concave surface of divergent branch leaves, but these are commissural rather than free pores.1 5.
